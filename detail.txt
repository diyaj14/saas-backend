PRD – CrashGuard (Algorithm Crash Insurance)
1. Product Overview
1.1 Vision
CrashGuard is a SaaS that protects creators from platform risk – especially sudden changes in algorithms and monetization – on major social platforms.

v1 platforms: YouTube, Instagram, LinkedIn.

CrashGuard:

Detects abnormal drops in reach/engagement that look like algorithm or environment changes, not “you just posted bad content”.

Quantifies how over‑dependent a creator is on each platform.

Generates concrete diversification playbooks (social → email, cross‑posting, content mix) so the creator’s business survives platform swings.

1.2 Problem
Creators and expert solo‑founders build audiences on platforms they don’t control:

A single algorithm update, policy change, or payout tweak can slash reach or revenue overnight.

Most tools (Hootsuite, TubeBuddy, Beehiiv, etc.) are focused on posting, SEO, or email, but none take responsibility for “you are over‑dependent on X and algo risk just went up.”

Creators know they should build email lists and diversify, but they:

Don’t see risk quantified.

React late, only after big drops.

Don’t get concrete, data‑driven “do this for the next 30 days” plans.

1.3 Product thesis
Treat platforms as black boxes, monitor their outputs like SREs monitor latency, and act as an AI strategist that helps creators rebalance their “audience portfolio” before algorithms wreck their income.

2. Users & Use‑Cases
2.1 Target user (v1)
Expert/B2B creator / “solo business”:

Examples: educator, consultant, indie dev, cohort‑course creator, coach.

Active on YouTube, Instagram, and LinkedIn.

Monetizes through courses, coaching, SaaS, or info products.

Has or wants an email list (Kit/ConvertKit, Beehiiv, Substack).

2.2 Secondary user
Creator managers or small agencies managing multiple creators.

Small brands relying heavily on organic social reach.

2.3 Key use‑cases
Early warning

“Tell me as soon as YouTube or Instagram starts suppressing my content abnormally.”

Platform dependency view

“Show me how much of my audience/income depends on each platform.”

Actionable diversification plan

“If IG crashes tomorrow, what exactly should I do for the next month to protect revenue?”

3. Goals and Non‑Goals
3.1 Goals (v1)
Detect & alert on anomalous drops in reach/engagement on YouTube, IG, LinkedIn that are likely platform/environment changes.

Compute a platform dependency score and overall platform risk index for each creator.

Generate 2–5 concrete diversification actions per alert, especially:

Social → email list growth

Cross‑posting to healthier platforms

Content mix adjustments

Support at least one major email provider integration (Kit/ConvertKit or Beehiiv).

3.2 Non‑Goals (v1)
No full scheduler (Buffer/Hootsuite replacement).

No thumbnail/SEO optimizer (TubeBuddy/vidIQ replacement).

No full email service provider (ESP) or CRM.

6. Anomaly Detection & Classification (Production‑Style)
This is the “how do you detect anomalies?” piece your CTO asked.

6.1 Step 1 – Normalize metrics
For each creator & platform, convert raw counts into normalized metrics:

Adjust for posting frequency and follower count.

Example metrics:

YouTube:

views_per_video, impressions_per_video, ctr, watch_time_per_view.

Instagram:

reach_per_post (per reel/feed/story), engagement_per_1k_impressions, followers_delta_per_day.

LinkedIn:

impressions_per_post, clicks_per_impression, followers_delta_per_day.

This ensures we are not confusing “you posted less” or “you grew 5x in followers” with algorithm changes.

6.4 Step 4 – “Algo vs you” classification
After we detect a change, we classify it based on which metrics moved.

Case 1 – Distribution/algorithm issue

Quality metrics OK:

CTR around baseline

Watch time/avg view duration around baseline

Distribution metrics tank:

impressions_per_video or reach_per_post down strongly
→ Platform is showing your content to fewer people even though those who see it behave the same. Strong signal of algorithm/distribution change.

Case 2 – Content issue

Impressions/reach stable

CTR and watch time drop
→ Hooks/content/thumbnail likely weaker. We label this as CREATOR_CONTENT_ISSUE, not algorithm.

Case 3 – Monetization issue

Views stable

RPM or revenue per 1k views collapses
→ Payout change or ad market issue, not reach.

We encode these logical patterns as simple rules per platform.

6.5 Step 5 – Peer cohort comparison
To avoid over‑flagging “algo”:

Build cohorts per platform:

same follower band (e.g., 10–50k, 50–250k),

same region,

similar niche tags.

For a given candidate anomaly on a creator:

“How many peers show a similar metric drop (magnitude + direction) in the same 1–2 week window?”

If ≥ 30–50% of peers show similar drop → classify as PLATFORM_EVENT (likely algo/env change).

If not → keep as creator‑specific issue.

This peer confirmation step is what upgrades “weird metrics” to a “probable algorithm/environment change.”

6.6 Final label and hand‑off
Each anomaly becomes:

PLATFORM_EVENT or CREATOR_CONTENT_ISSUE, with metadata:

platform, metric, severity, supporting evidence (z‑scores, cohort stats).

This feeds into the risk engine and recommendation engine.

7. Platform‑Specific Rules (YouTube, Instagram, LinkedIn)
These rules sit on top of the generic detection logic.

7.1 YouTube
Derived metrics: views_per_video, impressions_per_video, ctr, watch_time_per_view, subs_per_1k_views, split by Shorts vs long‑form.

Rule Y1 – Distribution drop

CTR & watch time per view ~ baseline.

impressions_per_video z‑score ≤ −2 for ≥ 7 days.

Changepoint: new 14‑day mean impressions < 0.6 × old mean.

Peer cohort: ≥ 40% show similar pattern.

→ Label YOUTUBE_DISTRIBUTION_DROP → PLATFORM_EVENT.

Rule Y2 – Shorts vs long shift

Shorts impressions ↑ significantly while long‑form ↓ (or vice‑versa), with posting mix unchanged.
→ Format preference shift → suggestions about content mix.

7.2 Instagram
Using Instagram Graph API metrics per content type.

Rule IG1 – Reel distribution drop

Saves per impression and completion rate for reels ~ baseline.

reach_per_reel down > 40% vs baseline for 7–10 days.

Carousels/feed posts stay around normal.

Cohort: many peers see same.

→ INSTAGRAM_REELS_DISTRIBUTION_DROP → PLATFORM_EVENT.

Rule IG2 – Discovery collapse

Engagement per follower stable.

New follower growth followers_delta_per_day < baseline − 2σ for 14+ days.
→ Explore/Reels discovery changed.

7.3 LinkedIn
Rule LI1 – Visibility drop

Reactions/comments per impression stable.

impressions_per_post down > 40% vs baseline for ≥ 10 days.

Rule LI2 – Outbound click suppression

Reactions stable, but clicks_per_impression drops sharply across posts with external links.
→ Suggests algo change in how much LinkedIn surfaces outbound links.

9. Recommendation Engine (Rules + AI)
9.1 Rule‑based core (non‑AI)
Rules use:

Alert type (YOUTUBE_DISTRIBUTION_DROP, INSTAGRAM_DISCOVERY_DOWNGRADE, etc.).

Dependency levels (YouTube/IG/LinkedIn/email).

Creator constraints (time budget, products, existing email list).

Example rule:

If alert = YOUTUBE_DISTRIBUTION_DROP and yt_dependency = HIGH and email_list_small →

Recommend 30‑day plan with:

More explicit email CTAs on YouTube.

2–3 lead‑magnet videos.

Cross‑posting to LinkedIn feeds, etc.

Output: structured actions with tags like build_email, cross_post, shift_content_mix.

9.2 Where AI (LLMs) step in
AI usage v1:

Explainer & coach

Convert raw alert payloads into plain‑English explanations like:

“Your YouTube impressions per video are down 43% vs the last 30 days, but CTR and watch time are stable. 46% of similar channels saw a similar drop last week, so this looks like a distribution change, not a content problem.”

Summarise recommended plan (“For the next 3 weeks, use YouTube mainly to push lead magnets, and shift deep‑dive content to LinkedIn + email.”).

Playbooks & copy templates

Given actions + creator niche, generate:

Social post ideas per platform.

Lead magnet ideas.

Email subject lines and body samples.

Simple funnel sequences (“YT Short → email signup → webinar”).

So AI here is “growth strategist + copywriter”, grounded in the metrics and rules. The detection & classification logic itself is deterministic, not black‑box.

9.3 Future ML enhancements (post‑v1)
Later, add more ML:

Smarter anomaly detection: per‑platform forecasting models (Prophet, GBMs) and residual analysis.

Automated cohort discovery: embeddings + clustering to build better peer groups than manual bands.

Alert prioritisation: small model that learns which alerts drive user actions and tunes sensitivity per creator.

10. Data Sources & “Real‑Time” Considerations
10.1 Official APIs (main signal)
YouTube Analytics/Data APIs – views, impressions, CTR, watch time, subs, revenue metrics.

Instagram Graph API – reach, impressions, engagement, profile clicks, follower stats.

LinkedIn Analytics/Marketing APIs – impressions, clicks, reactions, follower stats.

All are free up to reasonable quotas; daily per‑creator polling + 90‑day backfill is well within quota for early‑stage scale.

No need for true second‑by‑second realtime; daily resolution is enough for risk detection.

10.2 Secondary signals (optional, later)
Official changelogs & blogs – YouTube Creator Insider, Meta/Instagram Newsroom, LinkedIn blog, creator‑economy newsletters.

Simple crawler that records “known platform events” and overlays them on charts.

Global panel / platform health – As userbase grows, median metrics across all creators per platform become a “platform index”.

Social chatter – optionally surface trending “IG killed my reach” topics, but as UI context only.
